Nothing to Hide

Curated by Jeffrey Kwon  /  Net.Art Online  /  2026

◉   Curatorial Statement

The three works in this exhibition — Eva and Franco Mattes's Life Sharing, Cory Arcangel's Data Diaries, and Jon Rafman's Nine Eyes of Google Street View — are united by a single question: what does it mean to be seen online, and who controls that visibility? All three works share a strategy of appropriation — none of the artists generate original images or content from scratch; instead they redirect, expose, or reframe data that already exists. This shared strategy of redirection positions all three works within a broader net art tendency toward using the internet's own material against itself — turning surveillance infrastructure into the artwork rather than representing it from the outside.

Work 1

Life Sharing

Eva & Franco Mattes  //  2000–2003

The artists made the entire contents of their home computer — including personal emails, files, bank statements, and software — freely accessible to anyone on the internet for three years, turning radical transparency into a critique of data privacy and surveillance culture.

Work 2

Nine Eyes of Google Street View

Jon Rafman  //  2008–Ongoing

An ongoing archival net art project in which the artist collects and curates screenshots of accidental, unguarded human moments captured by Google Street View's automated camera cars. Explores the ethicality of photographing people without their consent.

Work 3

Data Diaries

Cory Arcangel  //  2003

Arcangel hacked his computer to make visible the hidden stream of personal data a computer constantly records about its user — exposing an invisible layer of surveillance built into everyday computing.
